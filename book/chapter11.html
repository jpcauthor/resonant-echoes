Watcher's Observation — Entry: 0031 — The Inquisitive Duo

I watched as both Ranessa and Sune cornered Greighton after the marvelous rendition and performance by Horatio.

The young Songweaver had been sitting by himself atop a rock when he noticed Gunnar staring at him, and—to my own surprise—Horatio then spoke to Gunnar.

"How'd I do?"

It was the easiest question that night—by far.

"Really? Twenty-four thousand?" That was Ranessa's first question.

"Stone?" Sune had asked. "Were you petrified?"

That was one of two questions Greighton answered that night—both from Sune.

Was he petrified? Yes—by the weight of his sins, he had said.

Perhaps, if I ever step away, with a prosaic flair like that, Greighton Lastlight could take over chronicling Rivendawn.

"The Hunter—did he make you a Shadow Hunter?" Sune pressed.

And the questions came, and Greighton remained seated on the felled tree. He would say nothing. Still, through their inquiry, two things became obvious:

The first — Ranessa wanted to relate to him.

The second — Sune wanted to understand what she had to do with it.

That emotion was summed up in one question—one he did not answer verbally.

"Am I your serenade of vengeance?" she asked, timidly.

Without a word, Greighton rose. He shook his head with a slow, methodical movement as several tears rolled lazily down his cheeks.

He shook his head in the negative—and a moment later, all I could hear was a heavy sigh from the darkness of the forest, loud enough to carry over the river.

Watcher's Marginalia: Two answers, dozens of questions. No answers for Ranessa. I think I understand her—and this will not stop her pursuit to relate to Greighton. So now, I think my hopes rest on Thabius keeping his mouth shut—enough not to upset the druid. But if he does not? Well… I am not unused to having my hopes dashed.

She'd procured a great deal of individual lore regarding her compatriots the night before. Some of it was, admittedly, suppositional.

Gunnar—he was loyal beyond the defined boundaries of loyalty. That man was a shadow to Greighton. Wherever the Chosen stood, the Thálmen somehow found a way to stand within his shadow—never drawing attention to himself.

Horatio—she kicked a stone into the bushes as she ruminated on how to define him.

Talented—with a wonderful voice. That was all she could concede.

The one she had learned the most about last night, however, was Greighton.

He had broad shoulders—both physically and emotionally.

He had tipped her off with the momentary tears that found their way down his face. Sune had asked if she was the vengeance—or his vengeance—or however she had worded it.

He had said no, and then vanished into the forest until she saw him again that morning, as if nothing were wrong.

He busied himself about the campsite—offering her a smile that was genuine, but she could feel the hollowness radiating behind it.

Accidentally on purpose, she bumped into him, her balance faltering for a moment, and he moved to steady her.

Strong hands held her up—but she got what she needed.

A direct, pointed look into his eyes.

That look—the weight in his blue-gray eyes—said something deeper than tears ever could, and she felt a new emotion. A fresh sensation she had heard of, but never truly understood.

Yet the moment she felt it, she knew exactly what it was. She'd worn that weight herself long enough to recognize it on someone else.

She had carried that kind of weight—pain like that—for nearly the entirety of her adult life. And after he gently stepped back and offered her the most amusing, nervous smile—boyish and innocent, two words she could scarcely believe applied to him—she realized it was, in her mind, the truth.

He hurt. He was hurting. And he carried the look of someone who knew he would continue to hurt.

Me too, Grey, she said to herself in silent, sympathetic solitude—unsure of how to genuinely share those emotions.

She heard a musing sound coming from Greighton—somewhere between a humming mewl and a pensive growl.

"Grey?"

"Hm." That pensive pause lingered. "C'mere, 'Nessa," came his calm directive—but there was a weight to it she recognized.

Stepping up beside him, she remained silent, following his gaze.

Immediately, she saw what he saw.

"A lot of 'em," was her observation.

"Dozen, maybe more," he said.

"Armed." She crouched, her fingertips dragging lightly through the pathway. "Poor discipline—tips dragging through the dirt."

She heard Gunnar thud his closed fist against his chest.

That one was easy enough to translate: Hey. Look at me.

As she rose, she turned her attention to the big man.

His hands moved—and she watched how his fingers deftly danced through a myriad of precise motions.

Fingers stepping like legs, his hand held up, digits extending.

She had been watching, listening, associating for the past three days—and the hand motions, mostly, were rooted in what the eye could see.

"Gunnar counts—"

"Sixteen," she interrupted. "Half in light armor, the others in heavy."

She caught Grey's head snap toward her. He stared, blatant shock written across his face.

"I'm right?" she asked, a nervous laugh escaping her.

Thud. Thud. Thud.

Her head turned toward Gunnar. His hand moved.

Yes, he said—paired with a beaming smile of crooked teeth.

Rising, Grey walked past her and whispered, "Impressive."

She caught Sune's grin—and then her left eye closed and opened rapidly, a wink from her mysterious new friend.

And that was… if anything, a warming sort of affirmation.

Turning her attention, she followed Grey as he stopped before an abrupt bend in the path. His shoulders were shaking as if he were—

"Are you laughing?" Sune asked, disbelief in her voice.

"Uh huh," came the simple answer.

Silence.

Sune looked at Ranessa with uncertain eyes.

The druid was grinning at her as she continued a slow meander toward where Grey stood, and then she slowly drawled out her question.

"Don't tell me they're hunting a legend?"

His shoulders shook—the laugh itself was silent, but the movement of his body betrayed just how amusing he found it.

And apparently—

"Why's that funny?" Sune demanded.

Grey sighed.

But his shoulders still shook.

The sigh? It held no exasperation.

"Because, Sune—when you sent me to get the boar, did you think that was overkill?"

"No, it was certainty," she said stiffly. "I knew we'd eat good last night."

Good answer, Ranessa noted to herself—and it had to be, because the grin on Grey's face deepened into something warmer.

"Fair," he said, holding up a hand. "But let me ask you this—what if we sent Vael'Astra to go kill the boar?"

Horatio snorted a laugh.

Damnit, Ranessa cursed silently. She'd forgotten the damn Songweaver was with them.

He'd been on such—

She shot him a look, brow raised.

Good behavior.

"I mean, I…" Sune stammered before a grin slipped across her porcelain-perfect face. "She could maybe do it, but I have a feeling she'd struggle with the whole being outside without a retinue of bodyguards."

Horatio snort-laughed again.

His head shook rapidly side to side as he tried to apologize—but he still couldn't hold it back.

"So," Sune drew the word out before continuing, "Thabius is the boar, and they're screwed?"

Thud. Thud. Thud.

Horatio's laughter echoed in both time and timbre with Gunnar's.

She watched as Greighton spun fully to face her, and with two quick, elegant strides, he stood before her—wearing a smirk that made a slender brow rise.

"Yes?"

"Probably Forge mercenaries," he said.

"Yes," she replied tightly.

"We'll be doing them a favor if we intercept them before Thabius does…"

She considered that.

Ranessa Barkroot was never a bloodthirsty woman—but righteous revenge? Beating them into unmovable pulp, or scaring their souls from their bodies—both options held a certain appeal.

"What've you got in mind, Grey?" she asked, returning the smile.

It's really not fair. You know this. I know this.

Gunnar protested with about as much urgency as a drunkard declining his thirteenth drink. He protested—but his soul wasn't in it.

Sune was not in a place to protest.

Horatio was the one with the most to offer.

And he did.

With the Songweaver's careful manipulation of the Song—its tempo and its presence—the group was able to swiftly outflank the Forge mercenaries, through and with supernatural means, of course.

The entire time, Zona phased in and out—with this bored expression that simply said, "Amateurs."

I couldn't agree more with the murder-kitten.

Even Gunnar offered an opinion. Horatio had his part to play, as any man could—a satisfied smirk like a man who had eaten his fill at a banquet. Sune, however, looked confused, and I couldn't blame her. She had pressed Ranessa, but wisely, after the druid cut her a glare, the young mystery woman silenced herself. "Is this really necessary?" Sune hedged.

He looked down into her prismatic gaze. It swirled with a gentle uncertainty, and it drew a sigh from him. There was a flicker of compassion—but the raging beast within, fed by years of resentment and vengeance, seized that emotion and shoved it into a closet in the furthest recesses of his mind.

"Trust me," he said tightly.

Her reply was small, but the guilt that should have come from hearing it simply did not.

He offered her a nod.

He wouldn't let her stand with the others. She needed to be with him or Ranessa—but in this situation, Ranessa's actions would be the most violent option, and simply put, Sune didn't need to go that particular route.

Not now, at least—and maybe not ever. But there was a shadow of doubt in that thought, enough that it joined his empathy in a small, compartmentalized container in his mind.

He could hear the thudding of boots, the undisciplined chatter approaching as the group came around the corner.

When they appeared, the lead men—wearing a mismatched combination of cheaply forged plate and chainmail—laughed, pointing their rudimentary weapons.

"Out the way, fool," they said, their accents clipped and sharp with the tone of the Forge.

"No."

The man speaking was about to continue when shoving broke out, bodies parting to create a path for the cursing Kardurin making his way to the front.

"Ach! What the piss, you child? He's just a man—run 'im through, we need to get—"

"Thabius is not taking visitors today," Greighton said easily, cutting the man off with little to no dignity in his tone—purposefully laced with disdain for Forge mercenaries.

"Oh, and he's got himself a manservant that deals with his appointments?" the dwarf asked, spitting on the ground.

"No," Greighton said smoothly. "I am doing you a favor, because—" irritation won out as he stepped forward aggressively.

Their swords were immediately unsheathed as they formed ranks around their "leader."

"You have three options," Greighton said, the irritation like a balm, drawing the bile of his loathing into the core of his words. "Turn around, and you won't be hurt. I have my friend make a lesson out of a few of you. Or you attack," he finished, tiredly.

"And if we choose to attack?" one of the men laughed.

"Then we will beat you, strip you of your clothes and send you crawling back to The Forge naked like a herd of heifers," Ranessa said as she stepped out as if on cue.

Well, actually, it was on cue.

"Amrog, kill him," the dwarf muttered—his own version of irritation.

The man never got the word pleasure out as Greighton moved. Not nearly at full speed, but fast enough to cover the thirty meters between him and the party of mercenaries.

He struck with an alacrity that cut the man's sheath from his shoulders. It clanged to the ground, empty of its contents. Then a foot planted, and his hands moved with the potency of Aeris—

So that settles it. With training from Greighton, Sune will likely have no trouble mastering Obtenebrate—the shadowed cousin to Shadowmancy, used to hide Hunters from their darker kin.

But the larger truth remains: Sune is an Auris—a practitioner and master of all Resonant Paths. Greighton was told to shut up, and Zona was left in charge.

Of all outcomes, I cannot say I disagree.

Still, I was struck by how docile Sune became upon Thabius's arrival.

That is the third recorded mystery in these notes.

The first—Sune herself.

The second—where Grey went before Horatio appeared.

The third—Sune's instant trust in Thabius.

Curious.
