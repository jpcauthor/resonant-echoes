Watcher's Observation — Entry: 0001 — In Which Trees Overshare

The forest is terrible at keeping secrets. Drop a little blood and suddenly every root is a court gossip, and of course I hear everything.

Ranessa Barkroot's tragedy began the moment she was born under a particular moon. A distinct reality for her that life, in fact, had claws.

Claws — emotional, physical, and right now even ephemeral.

Then Jadsia arrived—silk, poison, and the self-assurance of someone who believes murder is a form of personal expression and art.

Not all monsters are bred in the grip of evil. But those who seek to nurture their corrosive souls? Well, yes, they are evil.

Ranessa simply hated her mother—and for good reason.

However, there would be another entrant into this early morning soiree of despotism, heroics, and wallowing in self pity.

Grey's entrance, naturally, ruined the drama. He doesn't "arrive"; he corrects errors. Jadsia is one.

She will insist survival was skill or fate. It wasn't. The Song simply isn't done with her—and I, sentimental fool, dislike wasted providential potential.

And that—is Ranessa boiled down to a single note in the song.

The Grove will recover. Blood is annoyingly efficient fertilizer.

Luxuries many took for granted—breakfast among them. Even with the morning's scant portions, her leathers still felt taut. She wasn't sure if it was the salty seasoning or her guilt that left her feeling bloated.

Her feet carried her through the dense foliage, lungs expanding with each deliberate breath through flared nostrils.

Even alone in the forest—her natural escape—she could hear the screams. She could feel the rush of panicked adrenaline. The screams from the frozen north were alive in the dewy forest.

Visceral fear hung in the air—one that clung sharply to Ranessa Barkroot's senses. Did others feel it as she did? The pungent stench of hopelessness, slick with desperation, coated her tongue with something foul.

She hoped they did not. She would not wish this honed awareness of fear on anyone.

But it was her burden—the living echo of her mother's machinations.

At first, she mistook it for her own fear, her own self-loathing—despondency wrapped in pity. But it wasn't. Resonance did not work that way.

It did not care.

It simply was—Resonance.

And the taste grew stronger. Her pointed ears cut through the clutter of the wild, past wind and leaf, until all that remained was the sound she knew too well: ragged breathing… and fear.

She didn't need to command her muscles to tighten. The groan of leather stretched over powerful thighs preceded her as she surged into a sprint through the forest.

The fear—sharp, panicked, raw—grew stronger. It offended her senses, yet guided her movement. The gasping breaths ahead, steady and equidistant, pulled her forward beyond the great trees.

Her leather-booted feet struck Terra, and Terra answered. It pushed back with equal force, meeting her stride with living resistance. The ground did not merely bear her weight—it welcomed it. Each thudding step was returned, amplified, as Terra rose to meet her heels, driving her onward—faster still.

Then it hit her—like northern ice water thrown across her face. The chittering, the obscene giggling threaded with gnashing—familiar sounds—should not, could not be this close to the Cradle… to the Coppice.

Her pace faltered. Terra no longer urged her forward; it steadied beneath her, wary, rising as if to shield her.

And then she saw them.

Six incarnations of malice, hunched over a corpse. The sharp, coppery stench of blood filled her nose.

Imps—the playthings of Shadowmancers—gorging themselves on the carcass of a deer.

This was no accident. This was a willful incursion—one that blackened even the fertile soil beneath it.

The gnashing. The giggling. The wet tearing of flesh, followed by the sickening sound of it being devoured.

It filled her with rage.

A rage that beckoned—urging her to loose it. To let it free.

No, she chastised herself—rage, anger—these were more than enough to fuel her hunger for destruction. There was no need for the monologue that always clawed at the back of her mind.

With a growl—feral, deep, like a bass drum rolling through the earth—she bent Terra to her will. Two imps, still dancing with strips of flesh and pelt dangling from their lips, were crushed together as the ground surged upward. Like clapping hands, Terra closed—and erased them.

The moment of surprise was gone. She reveled in it as the remaining imps did what they always did—mindlessly hurling themselves at the nearest target.

Like a prizefighter, Ranessa drove her clenched fist forward. The first imp, having turned itself into a missile, met her strike mid-flight. Its path—and its existence—ended the instant her fist tore through its soft body.

She planted her foot and spun. Her heel came down hard, without hesitation, crushing the squirming body beneath it. Bone and pulp gave way as she ground it into the earth.

The last imp hesitated—thinking, perhaps.

Ranessa dove as a ball of black ichor streaked past her, splattering against a nearby tree. She rolled, came up fluidly, and surged forward—her hand snapping out to seize the creature by its throat.

The rage swelled—billowing, stoked like a blacksmith's forge. Her eyes narrowed as it burned hotter, sharper—

Until Ignis stopped waiting for permission.

The imp in her grasp ignited, its form collapsing into ash as her fingers released it. She cast it aside with a look of disgust—though she could not tell if it was meant for the creature…

Or for the rage still seething within her.

Soft applause drifted from behind.

Ranessa turned, already coiled to strike, as shadows gathered and took form. A woman stepped forth—her very presence drawing sustenance from the death and fear saturating the air.

"Delicious," the woman said, ruby-painted lips curling into a smile that was neither pleasant nor welcoming.

"But…" she added, her pout edged with something far darker than playfulness, "those were my favorites."

A Shadowmancer.

Here.

The reality struck—and then widened—refusing to settle as gasping whimpers rose from behind her.

The woman—no, not a woman, the Shadowmancer—strode closer. Ten meters, perhaps less. Her form revealed just enough to entice, to taunt—but Ranessa saw only the eyes.

Cruel.

Inhuman.

Hungry.

"Oh my," she purred, slow and savoring, hips swaying as she stepped forward.

Then—a sharp crack split the air. Wood groaned, splintered, and gave way.

Ranessa's gaze snapped upward. The tree—its trunk eaten through by the imp's black ichor—tilted, then collapsed, ancient weight crashing down toward the Shadowmancer.

Ranessa grinned.

So did the Shadowmancer.

With a flick of her hand, the falling tree veered aside, tearing through brush as it crashed harmlessly away.

"Where was I?" she purred, as though the interruption were nothing more than a passing inconvenience. "Ah… yes. Daughter of the Coppice, isn't it? Fruit of the Arch-Druid's womb?"

A pause.

"Yes… sorry," she added, her tone flattening into boredom.

"I need to kill you."

Silver blades, trailing shadow, screamed through the air toward Ranessa. From where, she did not yet know—and did not have time to care.

She dove.

The heavy thunk of steel burying into wood sounded behind her—followed by a scream. A woman's scream.

Ranessa hit the ground at the Shadowmancer's feet, reaching—no, pleading—into Terra. First for survival. Then for control.

And beneath it all—

Rage.

Not beckoning now. Commanding.

Destroy her.

Terra answered.

The ground surged, rejecting the Shadowmancer where she stood and hurling her backward. But before her body could strike the broad trunk behind her, she unraveled—melting into thick, oily shadow.

It dissipated—and reformed.

Whole again.

Smiling.

Impressed.

"You are as good as The Forge claims," she nodded—and this time, it felt horribly genuine. "But… perhaps I'll raise your body as a servant afterward."

The Shadowmancer smiled.

Her left hand rose.

Ranessa dove the opposite way—and straight into the path of a silver blade wreathed in shadow. It drove cleanly into her shoulder.

The pain did not come as she expected.

Not at first.

Then she saw it.

Inky shadows seeped from the wound, spreading beneath her skin—cold, invasive, alive with intent.

She raged.

She burned.

She felt the shadow take hold—its frigid purpose coiling through her.

To kill her.

"Why?" a voice that sounded irritated, but like a Coppice teacher—the kind of irritated and disappointed voice when the children forgot to clean a mess.

The next sound was unexpected.

The Shadowmancer gasped—and her fear now—Ranessa could taste it on the back of her tongue with the other.

Good, the druid thought as darkness began to claw its way from the edges of her vision.

"Greighton," the woman said with the same way that one might damn or curse the most contemptable being in their life.

"Jadsia," he said—his voice unfeeling—but somehow wrapped in dismay.

Greighton.

Greighton? Grey? The Cradle's Shadow Hunter?

Those queries, while important, she mused as the darkness encroached more—would probably never be…

…Answered.

She felt a pull at her shoulder.

No—more than a pull. A suction. Then pain.

All three, at once.

Her eyelids parted, and she was greeted by little more than a silhouette blurred by haze. The pulling deepened. The pain followed.

A groan slipped from her as rapid blinks forced the world back into focus.

With a weak whimper, she turned her head.

A man knelt beside her—mouth pressed to her shoulder, drawing from the wound with steady, deliberate purpose.

"What are you…" she croaked.

He lifted his head.

She met his eyes—damning eyes. The last many ever saw.

But for her…

They were deliverance.

He turned and spat. Normally a grotesque act—but her mind, sluggish as it was, began to piece it together.

He had drawn it out.

"…poison," he said, voice neutral, yet touched with a quiet gentleness. "Got to it in time."

"Come. Sit up," he coaxed.

She didn't need to ask who he was.

Elurien Vael'Astra's Shadow Hunter.

The one man in all the Cradle fully commissioned to eradicate beings like the woman she had just faced.

"Who…" she began, not resisting as he guided her upright. Her head swam, dizziness settling over her senses like a weighted crown.

"Jadsia," he said calmly.

She blinked.

Jadsia—the Shadowmancer from the Crimson Citadel. The one—

"Grevon's pet."

Again, he finished her thought.

"Stop doing that…" she protested weakly.

She met his eyes—and grinned, even through the pain-clouded haze.

She reveled in his uncertain expression—internally, of course.

And she took a small, quiet joy in it.

He blinked, wiping away spittle and drops of her blood that clung to his lower lip.

"Doing…" He paused, uncertain—felt, not just seen. "What?"

"Finishing my thoughts. My sentences."

The way she said it should have irritated him.

It didn't.

He found it… refreshing.

He knew she understood what he was.

He hated that—loathed it. Anonymity was a dying art in the Cradle.

But he knew who she was, too.

And he should have reasserted control—should have grounded himself, drawn the line back into place—but the Indomitable damn him, he couldn't look away from her eyes. Green pools… no—portals.

"Shadow Hunter?" she asked, the question soft, uncertain.

"Grey," he answered immediately.

"Grey?" she echoed.

"Yes, Northern Expedition Commander Ranessa Barkroot," he said, rising to his feet with a faint grin. "Or would you rather I call you Ranessa?"

Her brows furrowed. Her pointed ears twitched.

He extended a hand.

He noted—quietly appreciated—the reluctant sigh that slipped past her lips as she took it and rose. She matched his height—impressive for a Veylen.

"What brought you here?" he asked.

"The same thing as you?" she replied, already peering past his shoulder, searching for the source of the earlier cries.

"I sensed Jadsia's presence." His lips tightened, as if to spit, but he restrained himself. "Hard not to." The words carried venom.

"No, I felt it—sensed it…" she began.

"Sensed what?" he cut in, impatience slipping through.

"If you'd let me finish," she snarled—controlled, but edged with something dangerous.

He did not apologize.

Instead, he inclined his head—just enough—and waited.

She continued.

"Fear. Then her screams. You didn't hear them?"

"No," he said simply.

"Where—"

"Here," came a meek, weary voice.

He turned sharply. Saw nothing—only a cluster of rocks pressed against the roots of a thick, ancient tree.

He stepped forward—

—and was stopped. Firmly, but gently, pushed aside as the druid moved past him.

The day had only just begun, and already it had taken on a… peculiar turn.

Not unwelcome.

"Where are—" Ranessa called, stepping toward the outcropping. She moved past the boulders—then stopped.

Her head tilted. The wound in her shoulder still bled—still wrong.

Greighton noted it. Filed it.

There was strength.

There was fortitude.

And then there was this.

Recently struck by a Shadowmancer's poison—wounded in a way that would leave most broken—and she pressed forward without hesitation.

Toward the unknown.

Impressive.

"Come on," Ranessa said, her voice gentle.

There was more softness in it than he expected—but beneath it lay something steady. Unyielding. The strength of it came not from Resonance, nor from the Song, but from her.

As if she were saying: Come out. I'll protect you.

Greighton considered that—how few ever dared to offer such a thing.

From behind the outcropping, he watched her take several steps back, hand outstretched in invitation.

"Are you okay?" she asked gently—though her body remained coiled, ready.

He recognized the stance. Prepared for anything.

But the tone—

That was not his way.

There was sincerity in her voice. A quiet hope.

When was the last time I sounded like that?

Too long, came the answer. Bitter.

"Yes," she managed, the word barely holding itself together.

He shifted his weight, missing nothing.

The weight of the blades across his back.

The wind moving east to west, stirring the leaves.

Then—

Silence.

His gaze found her.

Strawberry-blond hair fell past her shoulders, and she tilted her head with quiet uncertainty.

He had heard her voice—

—but her eyes held him.

Prismatic.

Swirling.

Color made manifest—red, blue, green—turning slowly, impossibly, within her gaze.

Eyes he had seen before.

Once.

In a dream.

A dream he had been told was… prophetic.

And now she stood before him. Those same shifting, impossible colors turning within her.

"You," she said.

And all he could manage was—

"You."

Before the world dropped out beneath him, and darkness claimed him whole.

Watcher's Observation — Entry: 0004 — The Felling of a Giant

The forest is quiet again, pretending nothing happened. It always does. The birds return, the roots drink, the wind hums a lullaby for the newly broken.

Ranessa Barkroot will call it victory. Grey will call it coincidence. I will call it rehearsal. The Song seldom performs its masterpiece without a few rough drafts.

Jadsia has slithered back to her mistress; she will spin this failure into a fable of patience. The Shadow-Hunter sleeps, dreaming of a girl he was never meant to find. And the girl? She is the note that does not yet know it will end the symphony.

I should leave it there, tidy and tragic. But honesty insists: I felt something when Grey fell—an echo I have not heard since the day the Citadel was rent by shadow.

The harmony trembled. Perhaps the world has begun to remember itself.

I debate—ought I tell the Councilor… pardon, the Councilor Prime—that it has begun?

No. Her reaction will be worth cataloguing in the raw.

The Grove breathes, the Hunter dreams, and the Song stirs in its sleep. I am still listening.

The forest's capacity for denial is admirable; if trees had shoulders, they'd shrug for a living. And yes, I heard the same tremor when Grey fell. I'm simply choosing to ignore it until someone else panics first.
