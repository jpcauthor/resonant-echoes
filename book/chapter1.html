Watcher’s Observation — 0001 — In Which Trees See Too Little and Share Much

The forest knew of her.

The evidence was visceral.

The trees cannot stop their dramatic gossip, like courtiers whispering in the Courts of the Cradle.

However—and I heard it clearly—the forest chattered and buzzed about a druid who sat atop a log… well, like a lump.

Ranessa Barkroot was simply trying to find solace—something she has never had. Born beneath a moon that meant nothing to anyone but me…

I know what her future holds. Her mother wants it so—the vainglorious Arch-Druid sees her daughter and the potential within as a means to strengthen her grasp on the Coppice.

The Arch Druid is a fool.

Her daughter’s pride alone dwarfs her mother’s—a minuscule drop of her mother’s hubris in comparison.

Also, her daughter’s keen sense of smell, her prescience of nature, and the simple fact of her innate brilliance told her, moments ago, that she is far from alone.

If only her mother were so keen to her surroundings. But more blood is to be spilled.


Watcher’s Marginalia: What is about to happen will set changes in motion, rewrite history, and bring people to their knees. I, of course, will be there to watch it transpire, catalog every moment, and keep my own hubris in check. But I will say this…
I do find it exciting when the Shadow-Hunter appears. It is a precursor to the interesting—arriving in even the most mundane of moments.

 


The ground, known to practitioners of Resonance as Terra, undulated beneath Ranessa's hand. Her fingers moved carefully in wave-like motions. The ground, like a drunken sailor, was seduced by the rhythmic motion and writhed in submission, writhing and falling as peb and dirt displaced.

The earth obeyed her will alone.

Fear has a recognizable scent. Even an emotional weight that makes it more heady, more desperate as it crawls into her nostrils—announcing itself like a thunderstorm rolling across the western waters.

She was no stranger to fear.

There was a buzzing in her ears—a foreign sound, nuanced, something she had never heard, nor thought the Song capable of producing.

The buzzing sent her running—a pace rivaled only by the wind itself.

Leather boots pressed silently into the ground, Terra pushing back, while the wind—her old friend, her comfort—drove her forward with the impetus of survival.

For whom? It definitely wasn’t Ranessa. But something—the buzzing in her mind growing louder with urgency—told her she would discover that soon.

And she needn’t wait long.

The buzzing stopped as she witnessed—more than saw—the coalescing of shadows. They wrapped around a heavy boulder and cast it aside like waste, with indiscriminate care.

The scream she heard, the source still unseen, spurred her into action—but it was the shrill laughter that chilled her blood.

A woman stood there, robed in shadow—black, void of anything—rippling hypnotically about her, a hand raised to strike.

Shadows.

The calculus was easy.

The scent of death lingered in the air, familiar and heady. Shadowmancy—the forbidden art—wrapped about the woman, pungent in scent, abhorrent in reality.

From within, Ranessa felt the Song drawing deep. She felt it—but couldn’t hold it. It cried for freedom, and finally—she let it tear free.

The ground beneath the woman erupted, only to draw a cackling laugh as she shifted into inky tendrils of shadow.

As quickly as it happened, the shadows melded back into pale skin.

“Jadsia…” Ranessa whispered.

The woman said nothing, only turned her malevolent gaze toward the outcropping of rocks. Darkness began to drip from her hands, sizzling and burning the ground as the black ichorous droplets twisted into—

Spiders.

A skittering mass surged toward the rocks. A familiar scream—the same as before—filled her ears, and the fear, that scent which had drawn Ranessa here, flooded her senses.

There was a burning, a tight heat that spawned rage unlike any she'd known.

It was a roar, but different from any she had ever released in combat against the Forge mercenaries. This one made nature go quiet—the trees stilled—as she crouched and launched herself over the edge of the rocks.

With a powerful crash, the ground exploded outward. The spiders were splattered or sent hurtling back toward the Shadowmancer—who only looked entertained.

“I wasn't expecting to play today,” the woman purred.

Ranessa wanted to say something witty, something dismissive—but in that moment, she felt an empty weight behind her. No scabbard. No scimitar—her weapon of choice—rested at her back.

She stood face-to-face, unarmed against a Shadowmancer.

The druid.

Her Resonance.

And a burning rage that wanted to consume—its hunger insatiable.

“I don’t like playing with my food,” Jadsia whispered, “but I’ll make an exception for the Arch-Druid’s daughter.”

And that rage within? It grew quiet—briefly—until, with another unfamiliar roar, she threw herself toward the Shadowmancer. The wind swirled, dissipating the shadows as rocks and stone, drawn from the earth, pelted the laughing woman.

No sword, no extension, no defense—just the wind and Terra.

But that scream—it was the kind of fear any soldier, any veteran of combat would recognize. Hope did not exist—only the encroaching veil of death’s kiss.

As she struck, her hands a flurry of blows against nothing, that building rage within her grew—but then so did something else. Her eyes narrowed, focusing with a disassociated fury.

She could see…

See the woman within the shadows.

She lashed out, Terra wrapping about her fist, and this time a new scream was released into the cacophony of violence.

The Shadowmancer staggered back, shock and anger flashing across her face.

“You bi—” she began.

But the druid gave her no quarter. As she advanced, she pushed, the soles of her boots keeping the Terra at bay while pressing the attack.

The flurry of strikes continued, rage filling her, visceral and ecstatic, driving her forward. Blow after blow landed—until something foreign coursed through the druid.

Pain.

She looked to her shoulder, eyes wide. The Shadowmancer’s fingers dug into her flesh, blood pouring out as black ichor-coated fingers withdrew slowly.

The laughter began again, and all the druid could do was watch.

The blood on the Shadowmancer’s fingers was suckled clean, the woman’s eyes widening with recognition—but the arrogance of a crooked sly smile crept across the alabaster skin of her face.

“I am doing the realm a favor,” were the words that came next—and Ranessa believed them.

And now her own fear mingled with the scent of—

“Let her go…” a voice intruded upon the moment, as if his words might somehow convince the Shadowmancer.

And a third ripple of fear filled Ranessa’s nostrils, a scent that smelled of the grave and…

She couldn’t place the last note as the shadowy figure stepped in front of her. Barely, through the haze of encroaching gray and darkness, she saw the Shadowmancer’s eyes widen in realization.

Surrender.

“Captain,” the woman said, as if the word were poison in her mouth—spat with all the disrespect she could muster.

“Jadsia,” the man replied in a neutral voice—devoid of emotion, missing nothing but the inflection of her name.

Darkness took her as the schnick of blades leaving their sheaths cut through the air. They glowed—a cold, ice-blue along their length, with traces of lightning coursing across their edges.

“Shadow—”

“…Hunter,” Jadsia finished.

A hissing sound followed, as the putrid scent of rotting flesh and dissatisfaction became Ranessa’s last grasp of her senses.

The smell of the Shadowmancer.

The pain and embarrassment of defeat.

Culminating in the resignation of death.

 


Watcher’s Observation — 0002 — In Which Trees Begin Gossiping Again


And this is where the interesting manifests in the flesh.

The Shadow-Hunter—both women’s symbiotic declarations were, at best, symbolic. But when the “Shadow-Hunter” arrived, tactical advantages shifted with an immediacy only seen after eating a bad bowl of porridge.

And to see Jadsia shudder and retreat? Instead of committing another heinous act of violence, she simply snapped into a cloud of shadows upon his arrival.

I have always found his entrances to be nearly as dramatic as my own.

The timing is beyond impeccable. It rises to the level of austere precision.

But now—we have a dying druid, an irritated Shadow-Hunter, and an unknown source whose fear still wafts through the air.

I find myself rather… eager to see how this paths its way toward resolution.
