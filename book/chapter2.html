Watcher’s Observation — 0003 - A shackled heart meets gratitude—and falls.


I shall keep this short. The arrival of the Shadow-Hunter says much.

His presence is no balm—it soothes nothing—but it does remove irritants. Thankfully, our paths have not crossed, lest I irritate the most renowned swordsman in all of Rivendawn.

Yet now, I get to document what happens when a fish is taken from water and told to walk.

This ought to prove… entertaining.

If I can even truly experience such a thing in a way that makes the heart of me bubble with—


Watcher’s Marginalia: Let us pretend I did not commit to vellum something so absurd as to “bubble” with… anything. Let us simply observe—and see the reality that the Shadow-Hunter, nor even I, saw as a precursor to what is to come.

 


Wispy tendrils rose into the early morning sky. The orange hue cast by the rising eye of the Indomitable set the scene into what the man might have called a rather beautiful morning.

A Shadow-Hunter less than ten kilometers from the Velatrium?

Concerning.

The Arch-Druid’s daughter crumpled in a heap, ragged breaths as Jadsia’s poison worked its way through her?

Unfortunate.

It was unfortunate it wasn’t her mother—but regardless of his feelings about Ranessa’s lineage, he had a choice.

Find the source of the screams.

Save Ranessa.

Oddly, it didn’t even require thought.

The druid had acquitted herself admirably against a far superior foe. She fought with a ferocity he had only seen once before—and that both thrilled and frightened him to the core of his bones.

Coming to rest on both knees, he gently laid the Veylen on her back. Her pointed ear, the matted locks, the garb of the Coppice—he knew what he was looking at. A druid. Veylen. Rare among her kind to fight like this.

Not the time. He had to—

He stopped thinking and did it.

Rolling the druid onto her side, Ranessa Barkroot—veteran of the Forge skirmishes—had endured enough.

He leaned down, pressing his lips to the wound. Suckling, sealing, drawing the poison out.

It stung in his mouth—burned, actually—but he spat it onto the ground, watching as it sizzled, destroying a small patch of Terra’s fauna.

A final draw—but this time it was only crimson fluid, coppery on his tongue, the stale scent of iron lingering in his nostrils.

He placed a hand against her leather-wrapped chest.

Her breathing had returned—ragged and desperate at first, her body struggling to reclaim control. He pressed his knuckles into her sternum and began to rub. There was nothing intimate in it—only an attempt to restore what was normal.

She stirred.

It began as a whimper, teeth grating.

Good.

It hurt.

His brow knit, the furrow deepening as color suffused her face.

Better.

It hurt badly—and she was angry.

His knuckles stopped their motion. Her breathing regulated—normal, precise.

He looked down at her, head canting slightly to the side—almost the curious tilt of a predator. She was—under the circumstances—a beautiful Veylen.

Then her eyes snapped open.

She went from beautiful to exquisite—tempered jade eyes locking onto him.

And just as quickly, she went from docile to angry—protective, struggling against his grasp.

She couldn’t. That was not possible. He knew it—and the look of recognition shifted into confusion.

The struggle stopped as quickly as it had begun.

“Shadow-Hunter?” her voice wheezed, trapped air escaping her lungs.

“I prefer Grey.” He offered a smile—but she would never know one thing.

It was genuine.

Grey watched her sway. Her eyes blinking slow--with a forced wide opening as if such an action would clear the fog.

“Ranessa…” His voice was barely a whisper, but purposeful. “Wait…”

He lifted a hand—careful, precise—and placed it against her wound. A purple mist began to gather around his hand and her shoulder. The cloud danced and spun, almost gleefully—the part of healing he loved most.

It always felt—resonated, even—like happiness as her flesh began to knit…

“You know my name?” her question interrupted his thoughts.

He continued, but his smile—something that still felt foreign, though welcome—never fully formed. By the look she gave him, he surmised it must have been a smirk.

“Yes. Ranessa Barkroot. Everyone knows who you are—don’t play coy, Druid of the Coppice,” he said, letting another pulse of violet escape.

“Commander of the Northern Expedition.” He bowed his head politely, already beginning to step away.

She needed space, and he intended to give it to her.

“I know who you are,” he added, with a respectful finality.

“Grey…” she said, her lips shaping his name as if testing it—probing for familiarity.

He watched her smile, then nod. Decision rendered, he assumed.

“Thank you,” a pause—then her smile, not a smirk, split across her face. “Grey.”

His control reasserted itself, but the coldness he reserved for others felt… less absolute.

“You,” he began, rising, “fought one of the most dangerous Shadowmancers—and almost won.” He extended a hand down to her.

He watched her take his wrist, feeling the surge of her power as she rose. Her strength—the Terra-firma within her—was undeniable. She did not need his help, but she took it. Ranessa Barkroot, thankfully, was an ally.

“What were you?”

“You mean you weren’t here for it too?”

“It? Too? What?”

The confusion that had faded returned in full, wrapped in uncertainty.

“The cries. The screams.”

“I thought that was you…”

“No.”

A shaky voice reached them.

Soft. Feminine. Resonant.

And it unnerved him.

“It was me,” the voice declared, almost disembodied—her words bouncing around the rocks she hid within.

Before he could do, say, or react in any way, Ranessa was already moving.

Damnit.

Is this what it was like dealing with himself? he wondered.

The druid was not as adroit on her feet as she had been moments ago. He watched as one foot crossed over the other—leading to a rather ungraceful stumble—but she caught herself against an outcropping. Finding her footing, she pushed forward again—only to vanish behind the rocks.

He could hear the druid’s voice. There was an impressive quality to it—a preternatural strength of leadership. She would bark orders and expect them to be followed, but there was more.

Behind that gentle coaxing, the command carried something else—something the Shadow-Hunter found enigmatic.

There was an unspoken promise in her pleas to the unseen presence.

I promise, her voice said. I will protect you.

And he believed her.

“I’m Ranessa,” he heard clearly.

He couldn’t decipher the mumbled response, but something within—a nagging pull at the back of his mind—drew him through a mental door he had thought long sealed.

“Sune?” Ranessa’s voice was growing louder.

“Sune,” Grey muttered—and in his mind’s eye, he saw the memory clearly.

Vividly—frighteningly so.

Strawberry-blonde hair. Prismatic eyes. A scared smile.

That vision—that memory—was decades old. But only her face… that was all he could see.

And the question—the question rang through his memory with the force of a war drum beaten into submission. It made confident legs weak—and when Ranessa stepped out from behind the rocks—

“Grey,” her voice snapped him back, like the crack of a whip, into the here and now.

His eyes turned toward the druid as she strode forward, her confidence and balance fully returned.

“Grey,” she repeated—concern threading her voice, though she remained undaunted. “This is…”

“You,” Grey whispered.

She was Chosen—like him. Her strawberry-blonde hair fell gently against her shoulders, a sackcloth top hanging loosely to her knees.

But it was her eyes.

Swirling prismatic portals that pulled Greighton Lastlight backward through time—

“You…” the girl echoed.

His mouth fell open as he stared.

Ranessa’s voice became a hollow echo in his mind as past, present, pain, and prophecy collided. He could hear her calling his name, but he couldn’t focus—as if she spoke through water.

“You’ll protect me, right?” the girl asked, stepping out from Ranessa’s shadow.

As another shadow overtook Greighton, the Shadow-Hunter collapsed to the ground—one final thought echoing through his mind.

Her.

 

Watcher’s Observation — 0004 — When a Buttress of Stability Is Felled by a Plea

I have known of Greighton Lastlight since his birth in the Forge.

The man does not understand failure. In fact, I suspect he is either an idiot or a savant—though I remain unsure which—because he refuses to fail.

Odds that would break others were, to him, as simple as peeling a potato. Insurmountable tactical problems—which is simply to say, death salivating at the doorway—were ignored with a glee only the psychotic or the blessed could sustain.

Mayhaps the boy—always a boy to me—is both.

But to see him felled by a question… a simple plea? A hope carried in Sune’s voice, as if she already knew the answer before asking.

Cataloging her will prove… intriguing. But they have neither felt nor noticed me today, so I will remain, and observe what the druid does with “Sune”—and the immobilized Shadow-Hunter.

Watcher’s Marginalia: I have seen tyrants fall—through hubris and daggers. I have seen the silver-tongued choke upon their own falsehoods. But never have I seen a former Captain—a Shadow-Hunter—felled so easily by gentility.
