As recorded by The Watcher, nine years after the Kneeling at the Riven · Entry 000 — The Farm at Dawn

It began with the sort of silence that pretends it has nothing to hide. The morning felt off—a quiver beneath the usual chorus, like a musician warming up in the wrong key. I can't prove it was resonance at first; perhaps it was only my nerves reminding me they still exist. But the air shivered, and something beneath it stirred.

Something… or someone. I had my suspicions even before I reached the fields, though I will refrain from stating them yet. One must pretend to be rigorous.

The abandoned farm sits two miles east of the old aqueduct, where the Cradle's planners once promised expansion before promptly forgetting about it. Vines now do the work they would not. The soil hums faintly when the wind passes across it—memory, not magic, though people do adore confusing the two. I had visited the place years ago. It felt like a wound then. It felt like a held breath now.

At first, I mistook the light for sun on frost. Then frost does not usually stand up. The glow gathered from the ground in a shape that suggested intent, or at least an earnest attempt at self-assembly. The Song buckled around it—nothing catastrophic, just a hiccup. Enough to make even me take notice.

What rose was not a deity, nor any ostentatious avatar of cosmic will. It was a young woman. Barefoot. Shivering. Clearly mortal. Or at least doing a convincing impression.

Nineteen, by the look of her. Though appearances in these matters lie like diplomats. Pale skin streaked with dirt, hair like light filtered through smoke, and eyes still trying to remember how seeing works. She took one breath—long, tentative—and folded to her knees, fingers pressed to the earth as though asking permission to exist.

Something answered her. Not audibly, mind you. The resonance of the field slanted toward her, not dramatically, but undeniably. Dead crops rustled, the wind steadied itself, and my internal metrics—which do not appreciate surprises—skipped half a beat. I rarely enjoy being proven fallible, but the world delights in such small cruelties.

I stayed away, unseen. Observing is safer than participating, and infinitely more dignified.

I wanted to step forward, truly. To explain, to guide, to pretend that intervention might matter. But it was always a fool's errand—one I've watched wiser men die attempting. So I remained where I was, in the margins where sense prevails, hidden enough to observe without becoming part of the story.

She whispered a name: "Sune." Whether she remembered it or simply chose it, I cannot say. The word vibrated oddly—like something recognizing itself.

Amnesia radiated off her, not as revelation but as bewilderment: no sense of place, purpose, or power. I have witnessed enough births—divine, mortal, and bureaucratic—to know the look of someone who has just arrived with no instructions.

Within minutes, the field resumed its previous harmonic shape, as though embarrassed by the incident. She rose slowly, clutching her arm, and turned toward the city whose citizens were already arguing over breakfast, blissfully unaware that their stability might now depend on the unsteady path of a girl reborn in a cabbage patch.

She walked toward the road, head tilted slightly—as if listening for instructions that would never come. Something had summoned her, of that much I am reasonably confident. Whether it was Greighton's echo or some deeper chord is a matter for future entries… assuming we survive long enough to write them.

The sun crested the horizon. Light touched her shoulders; frequencies aligned for the briefest, impossible moment. Then she smiled—a small, startled thing—and whatever the world had begun, it continued.

The Watcher's Marginalia: And thus the world awakens, mistaking its savior for a lost child. Typical.
